
This should not be an accountant website. It should feel like a one-person institute: part consulting practice, part public classroom, part decision lab.

Use his personal name as the master brand. His credibility is the product. An invented consultancy name hides the very thing that will win trust. The masthead should read something like:

**Justin Lachal**
**Independent accounting, costing and strategy advice for consequential decisions**

Or slightly warmer:

**Better financial judgment for real-world decisions**

His real differentiator is not just “30 years’ experience.” It is this combination:

* accountant’s discipline
* professor’s ability to explain
* strategist’s sense of trade-offs
* public-minded generosity
* willingness to show the work

That combination should shape the whole site.

## The values

I’d build the site around six values, and make each one visible in the content and design.

**Clarity**
Plain English. Explicit assumptions. No consulting fog.

**Generosity**
Real free tools, real spreadsheets, real checklists.

**Independence**
No hype, no vendor agenda, no pressure tactics.

**Public value**
Better financial decisions improve institutions, not just profits.

**Rigour**
Show working models, structured reviews, and disciplined thinking.

**Practicality**
Every page should answer: what problem does this solve, what do I get, what happens next?

For the ethics page, the cleanest backbone is the profession’s own set of principles: integrity, objectivity, professional competence and due care, confidentiality, and professional behaviour. I would translate those into plain English and make them part of his public promise. ([APESB][1])

## One important positioning choice

Because he wants to talk about audits, reviews, and public-sector-style work, the copy needs to be precise. ASIC treats registered company auditor status and ongoing obligations as formal regulatory matters, and ANAO uses “performance audit” for independent and objective assessments of operations and support systems. Until his licensing position is settled for any regulated audit work, the site should clearly distinguish statutory audit from consulting reviews, performance reviews, control reviews, and assurance-style advisory work. ([ASIC][2])

So the site language should say things like:

* post-implementation review
* performance review
* operational review
* finance control review
* assurance-style advisory
* independent review

And it should avoid wording that implies a formal statutory audit engagement unless that status is actually in place.

## The navigation

Keep the top nav simple:

**Home | Services | Spreadsheet Says | Resources | Public Work | About | Contact**

Use the footer for:

**Pricing | FAQ | Capability Statement | Privacy | Terms | Conflict and Independence**

That is the right balance: expansive in substance, compact in navigation.

## What the site is really selling

He is not selling hours. He is selling judgment.

He helps people answer questions like:

* Should we buy this or not?
* Should we outsource finance or build it in-house?
* Did this project actually deliver what it promised?
* What is this decision really costing us?
* What is worth measuring?
* What is a sensible strategy here?
* What does the spreadsheet say, and what does it miss?

That means the site should be organised around decisions and reviews, not generic labels like “advisory” or “business improvement.”

## The page-by-page blueprint

### 1. Home

The home page should do three jobs quickly: establish credibility, show the kinds of decisions he helps with, and prove that he gives away useful thinking.

The hero should have:

* a strong headshot
* a headline like **Better financial judgment for real-world decisions**
* a subhead such as:
  **Justin helps boards, councils, leaders and organisations make better decisions through practical costing, post-implementation reviews, performance reviews and clear strategic thinking.**
* two CTAs: **Work with Justin** and **Explore free tools**

Then the rest of the page should flow like this:

**Credibility strip**
Fellow-qualified accountant, 30 years’ experience, former professor of accounting at La Trobe University, public-minded adviser.

**Decision examples**
A short band of real questions:
“Caravan or motels?”
“Outsource finance or build the team?”
“Buy the software or wait?”
“Did the project deliver its promised benefits?”
“Which measures actually matter?”

**Service pillars**
Six cards:

* Costing and commercial decisions
* Post-implementation reviews
* Performance and governance reviews
* Strategy and decision support
* Finance function strategy
* Workshops and speaking

**Spreadsheet Says teaser**
This should be prominent. Show a thumbnail of a real model and a line like: “Run the numbers. Then talk about what the numbers miss.”

**How he works**
A six-step strip:

1. Frame the decision
2. Surface the assumptions
3. Model what can be modeled
4. Name what cannot be modeled
5. Recommend action
6. Review the outcome later

**Free resources preview**
Three most useful downloads.

**Public work preview**
Latest essay, latest model, latest case note.

**Pricing philosophy**
Transparent, modest, fixed-fee where possible, community and public-interest work welcomed.

**Final CTA**
“Bring me your decision” or “Book a short scoping call.”

### 2. About

This page should not just be biography. It should be a statement of intent.

It should include:

* his story in three parts: accountant, professor, adviser
* why he likes helping people think with numbers
* why he gives tools away for free
* how he works with clients
* what he does not do
* his ethics and independence statement

A particularly strong section here would be:

**What I believe**

* good decisions deserve clear assumptions
* public money deserves serious thought
* spreadsheets are useful servants and bad masters
* finance should help people decide, not intimidate them
* reviews should teach, not punish

Also include:

* downloadable CV
* downloadable one-page capability statement
* link to speaking/workshops
* contact CTA

### 3. Services overview

This page should be outcome-based, not buzzword-based.

Each service card should answer four questions:

* What is this for?
* Who is it for?
* What do you get?
* What happens next?

The six service areas should be:

**Costing and commercial decisions**
For buy/lease, make/buy, hire/outsource, property, capital spend, and other consequential decisions where a model helps.

**Post-implementation reviews**
For asking, after the fact, what worked, what did not, whether benefits were delivered, and what should change next time.

**Performance and governance reviews**
For controls, reporting quality, accountability, decision processes, and operational effectiveness.

**Strategy and decision support**
For messy issues where the numbers matter but do not settle the question on their own.

**Finance function strategy**
For designing or improving how finance work gets done: reporting, team structure, month-end, management information, outsourcing choices.

**Workshops, speaking and mentoring**
For boards, leadership teams, public talks, seminars, and training.

Each card should link to a full service page.

### 4. Costing and Commercial Decisions

This should be one of the flagship pages.

Headline: **Before you buy it, build it, outsource it, or shut it down, test the decision.**

This page should include:

* the kinds of decisions he helps with
* examples
* what a good model does
* what a model cannot decide
* sample deliverables
* how long a review takes
* fixed-fee options

The examples section should be vivid:

* caravan versus motels
* outsource finance versus build an internal team
* buy software versus keep the current system
* hold or sell a property
* employ versus contract
* standardise or customise

The deliverables should be concrete:

* one-page assumptions sheet
* working spreadsheet model
* short decision memo
* board-ready summary
* discussion session if needed

This page should also introduce his “A/B/C/D decision” idea as a signature framework. The labels can be refined, but the concept is excellent: some decisions are strategic and values-laden, some are judgment-heavy with models, some are repetitive enough that a spreadsheet should do the heavy lifting, and some are routine. That becomes his own IP.

### 5. Post-Implementation Reviews

This should be another flagship page because it is unusual and valuable.

Headline: **Once the project is done, find out what actually happened.**

This page should be framed as learning, accountability, and benefits realisation, not blame.

Public-sector guidance treats post-implementation review as checking whether planned benefits were delivered, documenting lessons, and improving future projects; one Victorian source notes the review is usually undertaken around 12 months after project completion. ([Victorian Auditor-General's Office][3])

So this page should explain:

* when to do a review
* what questions it answers
* what evidence he uses
* what the final output looks like
* how it helps boards, councils, NFPs and leadership teams

Sample output:

* benefits assessment
* lessons learned register
* strengths and weaknesses summary
* follow-up action plan
* board summary
* optional workshop to discuss findings

### 6. Performance and Governance Reviews

This is where he can house the more programmatic “little audit programs” he mentioned, without overclaiming regulated audit authority.

Headline: **Independent reviews of controls, reporting and organisational performance.**

This page should cover:

* finance control reviews
* reporting pack reviews
* process walk-throughs
* bank rec / cash control review
* aged receivables review
* budgeting and forecasting review
* KPI and performance-measure review
* grant or program governance review

What makes this page different is the method section. He can say the work is:

* checklist-based where appropriate
* evidence-based
* proportionate
* practical
* focused on improvement, not theatre

A smart addition here is a **Review Packs** section:

* Cash and bank review pack
* Receivables review pack
* Month-end close review pack
* Forecast and budgeting review pack
* Performance measure review pack

Each pack can have a free summary checklist and a paid facilitated version.

### 7. Strategy and Decision Support

This page should lean into his plain, no-nonsense strategy style.

Headline: **Simple tools for difficult choices.**

This is not abstract consulting strategy. It is applied strategy for real decisions.

The page should cover:

* force-field analysis
* 80/20 prioritisation
* option framing
* scenario thinking
* finance strategy
* board decision support
* leadership coaching on hard choices

This page should sound humane and grounded:
“Real decisions are rarely settled by one ratio. They need a sensible model, a clear conversation, and an honest look at trade-offs.”

Possible deliverables:

* half-day strategy workshop
* decision briefing note
* option comparison framework
* facilitation session
* follow-up memo

### 8. Finance Function Strategy

This should sit under services as a distinct page because it is easy to understand and buy.

Headline: **Build a finance function that helps people decide.**

Questions this page can speak to:

* Should finance be in-house or outsourced?
* What should the reporting pack look like?
* Which KPIs matter?
* How should month-end work?
* What does a sensible planning cycle look like?
* What should a lean finance team actually do?

Outputs:

* finance diagnostic
* target operating model
* reporting redesign
* role clarity
* 90-day improvement plan

This page will likely win work because it is concrete.

### 9. Workshops, Speaking and Mentoring

He should absolutely use his professor background here.

Headline: **Teaching, workshops and public talks on finance, decisions and strategy.**

This page should offer:

* board workshops
* leadership offsites
* short courses
* public-interest talks
* mentoring/coaching
* university-style seminars for practitioners

Topics could include:

* better decisions with simple financial models
* what post-implementation reviews teach
* lessons from financial history
* finance strategy for non-finance leaders
* what a board should ask before approving major spend

This is also the right place for his public-facing ambition.

### 10. Spreadsheet Says

This should be a top-level menu item, not a buried blog category.

This is the most distinctive idea in the whole concept.

The format of each entry should be consistent:

* the question
* the assumptions
* the model
* what the spreadsheet says
* what the spreadsheet cannot say
* what might change the answer
* download the file
* hire Justin to adapt it

Launch this section with at least five entries:

* Caravan versus motels
* Outsource finance versus internal team
* Buy now versus wait
* Own versus lease
* Property hold versus sell

This section is the bridge between his generosity and his consulting. People come for the free thinking and stay for the judgment.

The resource categories should be:

### 12. Public Work

This page is what turns the site from brochure into institution.

Headline: **Working in public**

It should include:

* current questions he is exploring
* public notes
* de-identified case reflections
* public-interest projects
* open office hours or Q&A
* “what I’m thinking about now”

A strong line for this page would be:
“I publish methods and models because better decisions should not be trapped behind a sales conversation.”

### 13. Case Studies

Case studies should be written like mini teaching cases, not chest-beating sales blurbs.

Structure each one as:

* context
* question
* what we looked at
* what changed
* lesson

If confidentiality is tight, label them clearly as de-identified or illustrative.

Launch with three:

* a costing decision
* a post-implementation review
* a finance function improvement case

### 14. Pricing

He wants transparency, which is a strength.

But I would not lead with **$100/hour** on the homepage. That makes a senior expert look underpriced and can trigger distrust rather than trust.

He should position himself as **accessible, not cheap**.

The pricing page should show fixed-fee pathways such as:

* Quick question / office hour
* Spreadsheet review
* Decision memo
* Review sprint
* Workshop day
* Full review project
* Ongoing advisory

It is fine to say:

* transparent fixed-fee options
* modest rates
* community and public-interest concessions
* scoped to fit the problem

That keeps the generosity without devaluing him.

### 15. Contact

The contact page should feel like intake, not just a generic form.

Use four pathways:

* I need help with a decision
* I need a review
* I want a workshop or talk
* I want to discuss a public-interest/project idea

The form should ask:

* what decision or review is involved
* who the organisation is
* timing
* what documents already exist
* whether they want a call or written response first

A file-upload field would be very helpful.

## What will make the site memorable

Three things.

First, **Spreadsheet Says** as a branded public resource.

Second, **working in public** as a visible philosophy, not a side note.

Third, **sample deliverables** everywhere. Show the first page of a decision memo. Show the assumptions sheet. Show the checklist. Show the spreadsheet screenshot. Let people see what “good” looks like.

Most consultant sites hide the work. His should show enough of the work to make trust easy.

## What he should give away for free

This part matters because it matches his character.

At launch, I would give away:

* 3 spreadsheet models
* 3 review checklists
* 2 decision templates
* 4 short essays
* 1 public talk or webinar recording
* 1 reading list on finance, public value and strategy

Within six months, grow that to:

* 10 spreadsheet/model resources
* 10 checklists or review packs
* 10 essays
* quarterly public talk notes
* a small newsletter archive

## How the site should look

Visually, this should not look like a suburban tax accountant or a glossy corporate consultancy.

It should feel:

* editorial rather than salesy
* warm rather than cold
* serious rather than flashy
* human rather than corporate
* practical rather than abstract

Design choices I’d make:

* strong portrait photography
* screenshots of real spreadsheets and memos
* lots of white space
* serif headings with clean sans-serif body text
* muted, scholarly colours
* no handshake stock photos
* no skyscrapers
* no faux “innovation” language

Think: professor’s study meets public-interest consultancy.

## How expansive it should be

Expansive in ideas, not in navigation.

For launch, I would build:

* 10 to 12 core pages
* 5 service subpages
* 1 flagship Spreadsheet Says hub
* 1 resource library
* 3 to 5 downloadable models/checklists
* 3 case notes
* 4 to 6 essays
* 1 capability statement PDF

That is enough to feel substantial and credible without becoming a content graveyard.

Then expand by publishing, not by adding more navigation.

A sensible rhythm is:

* one Spreadsheet Says piece per month
* one essay per month
* one new checklist or model every 6 to 8 weeks
* one public talk or note each quarter

## Build it so it scales

From a content architecture point of view, the site should have reusable content types:

* Service
* Resource
* Spreadsheet model
* Case note
* Article
* Workshop/talk
* FAQ

Tag everything by:

* audience
* topic
* format

That way one new piece of content automatically appears in the right places across the site. The site grows like a library, not like a pile of disconnected pages.

## One extra thing I would absolutely add

A **Capability Statement** download for councils, boards, universities, and public organisations.

One page. Clean. Printable. Includes:

* who he is
* service areas
* relevant background
* how he works
* procurement/contact details
* insurances/compliance details if needed
* link back to site and resources

That one document can win institutional work.

## What to avoid

Do not make it look like a general accounting firm.

Do not bury the free tools.

Do not lead with hourly pricing.

Do not use “audit” loosely.

Do not fill it with vague promises like “tailored solutions” and “unlocking value.”

Do not launch with empty blog categories and no real substance.

Done right, this site will feel less like a consultant brochure and more like a small public institution built around one person’s judgment.
